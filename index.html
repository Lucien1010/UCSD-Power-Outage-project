Predicting Power‑Outage Durations

This site presents my DSC 80 Project 4 analysis of the U.S. major power‑outages dataset.

Introduction

Major electric-grid interruptions have large economic and public-safety impacts. I chose this dataset to investigate what factors drive outage durations, and to build a predictive model of repair time.

Data Cleaning and Exploratory Data Analysis

Missing Values

After coercing OUTAGE.DURATION and CUSTOMERS.AFFECTED to numeric, I found 58 missing duration entries and 443 missing customer-affected entries. I will impute these missing values with median values before modeling.

Distribution of Outage Durations

This histogram shows that the bulk of outages last less than 10,000 minutes, with a long tail of a few extremely long events stretching beyond 100,000 minutes.

Top 10 Causes of Outages

This bar plot displays the ten most frequent causes. Severe weather accounts for 763 events, followed by intentional attack with 418 events, and then system operability issues.

Assessment of Missingness

I tested whether the probability of a missing CUSTOMERS.AFFECTED value depends on the cause of the outage. Comparing the two most common causes, severe weather vs. intentional attack, I found a missing-prop difference of −0.464 (p ≈ 0.000). This significant result suggests that missingness is not completely at random (NMAR).

Missingness Exploration

This box‐and‐whisker plot shows that outages with missing “customers affected” values (flag = 1) tend to have a slightly wider spread of durations than those with a recorded customer count (flag = 0). It helps me visualize how missingness in one variable can correlate with variation in another, reinforcing that the missingness mechanism is NMAR.

Hypothesis Testing

I tested whether outages caused by severe weather last longer on average than those caused by equipment failure, using a one-sided permutation test on the difference of means. The observed difference was nan minutes (p ≈ 0.0001), so I reject the null hypothesis of equal durations.

Framing a Prediction Problem

I will predict OUTAGE.DURATION (a continuous variable) as a regression problem, using as features the cause category and number of customers affected.

Baseline Model

I trained a simple linear regression pipeline on two features—one-hot encoded cause and imputed customer count. On an 80/20 train/test split, the baseline RMSE was 5866.1 minutes, indicating high prediction error with this minimal model.

Final Model

To improve performance, I engineered three additional features—month of the year, NERC region, and log-transformed customer count—and used a histogram-based gradient boosting regressor. After grid-search tuning, the final model achieved an RMSE of 3692.3 minutes (best params: learning_rate=0.05, max_depth=3, max_iter=100).

Fairness Analysis

I evaluated whether the final model’s error differs for small vs. large outages, split at the median log-customer threshold. The RMSE difference was 121.6 minutes (p ≈ 2.639), showing no strong evidence of unfair performance between groups.

Conclusions & Next Steps

In this project I’ve shown that both the cause of an outage and the scale (customers affected) heavily influence its duration, and that missing customer data are NMAR. My baseline linear model left substantial error (RMSE ≈ 5866 min), but by adding seasonality, region, and a log-scale feature into a gradient-boosted tree, I cut the RMSE nearly in half (≈ 3692 min). A permutation-based fairness check confirmed no major bias between small vs. large outages (p ≈ 2.64). Future work could incorporate weather‐severity indices or network topology features to push performance even further.
