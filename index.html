Predicting Power‑Outage Durations

This site presents my DSC 80 Project 4 analysis of the U.S. major power‑outages dataset.

Introduction

Major electric-grid interruptions have large economic and public-safety impacts. We chose this dataset to investigate what factors drive outage durations, and to build a predictive model of repair time.

Assessment of Missingness

We ran a permutation test on whether missing CUSTOMERS.AFFECTED depends on cause category (comparing the two most common causes). The p-value was < 0.01, so missingness is MAR, and we imputed it rather than ignore it.

Hypothesis Testing

  • Severe Weather vs. Equipment Failure: Severe‐weather outages last on average 166 minutes longer (p < 0.001) → reject H₀.
  • Summer vs. Winter Repair Rates: Only ~43 % of summer outages fixed within 120 min vs. ~58 % in winter (Δ≈–0.15, p≈0.003) → reject H₀.

Framing a Prediction Problem

We’re predicting OUTAGE.DURATION (a regression task) using features that would be known at outage start.

Baseline Model

Linear regression on just cause category (one‐hot) and customers affected gives an RMSE of ~190 minutes on hold‐out data.

Final Model

After adding month, NERC region, and a log-transform of customers, and switching to a histogram-gradient-boosted regressor (tuned via grid‐search), RMSE falls to ~170 minutes (~10 % improvement).

Fairness Analysis

Comparing RMSE on large vs. small customer events (split at the median) yields no significant difference (p≈0.48), so the model is fair across event sizes.

Conclusions & Next Steps

This work shows that severe weather and summer both prolong outages, and that enriched features can cut prediction error by 10 %. Next, we could add real‐time weather data or crew‐dispatch logs to push RMSE even lower.
